Problem

You can't safely teach an emergency by causing one.

The oil and gas industry continues to lose more than ten Canadian workers each year to on-site fatalities — the majority traced back to simple mistakes or unfamiliarity with equipment during high-consequence events.

Traditional training has a structural problem: classroom-only instruction lacks practical engagement, and on-site training introduces real safety risk and operational disruption. Compressor startups and emergency shutdowns can't be rehearsed on live equipment without putting people and product at risk.

The brief

Build an immersive, scalable training environment that lets operators make high-consequence decisions, repeatedly, without consequence — and measure whether they're actually getting better.

02

The VR simulation.

Unity · Meta Quest · WiFi-tethered hardware

A two-stage compressor station you can walk through.

The trainee enters a Unity-built compressor station rendered for Meta Quest using the Meta XR SDK. As they advance through a 12-step startup procedure, a state machine validates every action against the SOP — scrubbers, compressors, intercoolers, recycle paths, and the export line, all rebuilt at the scale operators experience in the field.

Locomotion is teleport-based — fixed nodes at suction, interstage, and export — to eliminate motion sickness and keep trainees focused on procedure, not their stomach. UI panels are spatially anchored but resizable, so they don't obstruct equipment during critical steps.

Live process feedback you can read at a glance.

Pipe segments dynamically change colour based on the current pressure state at every monitored stream — grey for no flow, yellow below range, red above range, and green when conditions are valid for the active step. Compressor audio scales with dial position and intercooler activity is signalled by flashing status lights.

Pressures are pulled from a precomputed lookup table of 100,000+ rows generated by an Aspen HYSYS case-study sweep — O(1) runtime with no simulation delay. The trade-off was deliberate: prioritise stable VR performance and procedural fidelity over high-frequency transient prediction.

Hardware

A control panel you can actually feel.

The literature was clear: tangible interaction beats hand-tracking for procedural training. Trainees who turn real switches build sustained proficiency over time; those who use VR controllers don't. So we built the panel.

  • 14 rotary encoders — infinite rotation, digital stepping for 0–100 % valve control
  • 4 industrial push buttons — momentary, mirroring real motor-starter logic
  • 1 latching mushroom-head ESD — physically locks open until twisted to reset
  • ESP32-WROOM-32E + MCP23017 GPIO expander, talking TCP-over-WiFi to Unity on port 7777
  • Modular shell — 3-section PLA chassis with M5 brass heat-set inserts for repeated disassembly

Inputs flow through a centralised InputState registry that decouples raw TCP messages from procedure logic — meaning the same 12-step state machine validates either the physical panel or a software input simulator built into the Unity editor.

04

The AI instructor.

RAG · GPT-4o-mini · ChromaDB

An instructor that cites its sources.

Live human instructors don't scale, especially for the rare emergency-response drills the simulator is designed for. So we embedded an AI instructor inside the VR experience — the trainee speaks to it by voice, speech is captured, transcribed, and routed through a Retrieval-Augmented Generation pipeline.

Relevant SOP passages, equipment manuals, and safety guidance are retrieved from a vetted knowledge base before generation. The model is constrained to answer only from retrieved context and to decline when no relevant passage exists — important in a safety-critical context where a black-box assistant isn't acceptable.

  • nomic-embed-text via Ollama — local embeddings, no API cost on indexing
  • ChromaDB as the vector store
  • GPT-4o-mini for generation, target latency < 5 s per query
  • FastAPI on UCalgary CloudStack, behind Nginx, as a persistent systemd service
  • SOP Manager in the admin dashboard — coordinators upload DOCX / PDF; chunked & embedded server-side without dev involvement
05

System architecture.

VR · Hardware · HYSYS · RAG · Cloud

The architecture deliberately separates procedure logic, process-response data, and VR assets — so the platform extends to new training scenarios without rewriting the simulator.

Admin Dashboard

Cohort screening on top, step-level diagnosis below.

A two-tab Streamlit application reads from PostgreSQL via REST. The upper tab is for cohort-level monitoring — aggregated trends, top-performer / most-errors auto-surfacing, plus the SOP Manager for managing the AI instructor's knowledge base.

The lower tab drops into a single trainee — completion time per step, colour-shaded by correct / incorrect execution, with overlapping session plots so recurring weaknesses become visible across attempts. You can tell instantly whether someone is slow but accurate, or fast but stuck on the same step.

Outcome

Third place out of 149 teams at the UCalgary Capstone Design Fair.

VR OPS landed on the podium against the largest cohort the fair has run. It produced a working VR client, a tangible control panel, a deployed cloud-hosted AI service citing its sources, and a coordinator dashboard already viable for cohort-scale training — all addressing a real industry gap: rehearsing the high-consequence faults that live equipment can't safely teach.
