Project · 03 Carbon Fiber Manufacturing Lab May 2023 — Dec 2023 Schulich Research Award

Microwave-plasma carbonization of asphaltene precursors.

Eight months in the Carbon Fiber Manufacturing Lab — sourcing asphaltenes, carbonizing them through to fiber, and benchmarking a homebuilt microwave-plasma activation rig against the furnace and laser-heating methods it was meant to replace.

Role Research Intern
Lab Carbon Fiber Mfg. Lab · UCalgary
Funding Schulich Research Award
Method Microwave plasma vs furnace · laser
Source material Asphaltenes
Premise

Could a plasma do what a furnace does — better, cheaper, faster?

Conventional carbon-fibre production runs precursor tow through enormous graphitization furnaces at temperatures north of 2,000 °C — slow, expensive, and inefficient. The lab's question was simple: could you activate the same material faster, with less energy, using something other than a giant oven?

Conventional methods have known trade-offs. Furnace heating is uniform but slow and energy-intensive — my own SolidWorks thermal model of the lab graphitization furnace mapped 15–20 % temperature non-uniformity across the tow bundle, linked directly to inconsistent fiber microstructure. Laser heating is fast but spatially restricted, with significant beam-edge artifacts.

The hypothesis: a microwave plasma could couple energy directly into the precursor in a way that's more uniform than a laser and more efficient than a furnace — if you could build a stable reactor on a research budget.

02

Yes, the microwave is from Walmart.

Approx. CAD $89 · 1100 W

A research-grade microwave plasma source costs tens of thousands of dollars and arrives on lab procurement timelines measured in months. We didn't have tens of thousands of dollars. So I did what you do.

I drove to Walmart, bought a consumer microwave, harvested the magnetron and waveguide, and rebuilt them into a benchtop reactor cavity — designed to host the asphaltene precursor under controlled atmosphere while the magnetron drove a stable plasma discharge above the sample.

It looked exactly as crude as it sounds — and it worked. Once it lit up, the plasma column was stable enough to run repeat trials. The benchmark numbers for the comparison study against the furnace and laser methods came out of this rig.

The story behind the rig

"If a $40,000 instrument and a $89 microwave both fire a plasma that can carbonize a precursor, the microwave is the better instrument — because you can build five of them, break two, and still keep researching."

03

Three methods, side by side.

Furnace · Laser · Plasma
Furnace · uniform but slow · 15–20 % cross-section non-uniformity
Laser · fast but spatially constrained · edge artifacts
Plasma · homebuilt · benchmarked vs the other two

The plasma rig was benchmarked head-to-head against the lab's existing furnace and laser stations on three axes — energy efficiency, activation uniformity, and resulting fiber tensile properties. That meant feeding identical precursor batches through all three methods and pulling the post-treated fibers through the same QA pipeline so the comparison was fair.

The earlier SolidWorks thermal model of the graphitization furnace wasn't decoration — it directly shaped how we read the numbers. Knowing the furnace had a 15–20 % thermal non-uniformity baked in meant variability in the furnace samples wasn't process noise to be averaged away, it was a known signal. The plasma comparison asked the right question: not "is plasma faster?", but "does plasma reduce the variance the furnace can't eliminate?".

The Walmart microwave is the punchline of the story; the discipline of comparing it fairly to a $40,000 furnace is the actual work.

04

End-to-end through the pipeline.

Sourcing → reporting
Sourcing asphaltenes

Selected and sourced asphaltene feedstocks from Albertan oil-sands streams; characterized starting material to set process baselines.

Refining

Solvent extraction and filtration to isolate the carbon-rich fraction and prepare it for thermal processing.

Carbonization

Inert-atmosphere heating drives off non-carbon volatiles — the controlled pyrolysis step that locks the carbon scaffold.

Graphitization

High-temperature treatment to align graphitic crystalline structure — where the furnace's 15–20 % non-uniformity showed up directly in fiber microstructure.

Fiber formation

Spun and drew the activated material into continuous tow — the form factor used for downstream mechanical testing.

Tensile QA

Mechanical testing of the resulting fibers — tensile strength, modulus, failure mode — across furnace, laser, and plasma-treated samples for direct comparison.

Microscopy

Optical and SEM-level inspection on cross-sections to verify microstructural uniformity and tie tensile variance back to thermal exposure.

Reporting

Wrote up the comparative results with revised zone-heating setpoints and ramp-rate recommendations for the lab's furnace recipe.

Takeaway

Build the rig. Trust the microscope, not the mood.

The eight months in the lab taught me how much engineering work happens before the data exists. Sourcing, refining, building a reactor that survives a hundred runs — that's the project. The graphs come at the end. The plasma comparison demonstrated meaningful gains in activation uniformity relative to the furnace baseline at substantially lower energy input — an encouraging direction for follow-up work, with the obvious caveats around a benchtop self-built rig.
