Executive Summary

         Virtual reality is gaining popularity for use in the oil and gas industry for training
purposes. In recent years, there has been a growing trend toward utilizing virtual reality
technology to create interactive learning environments for training workers. One area where
virtual reality has shown great potential for improving worker training is in the oil and gas
industry. Training for workers who operate compressors at various stages of the natural gas
production process may be particularly well-suited for development using this type of interactive
computer technology.

         In particular, training for the operation of compressor stations during the startup phase of
compressor station operation could benefit from the use of a fully functional, user-interactive
virtual-reality simulator. During compressor station startups, operators are required to make
many decisions rapidly, such as when to start up the unit safely. It would be highly beneficial if
those decisions could be made under controlled conditions prior to actually making them during
actual operational events. As an example, one area of study that could utilize a fully functional
VR simulator is the design and development of two-stage centrifugal compressor stations used to
compress high-pressure Montney natural gas.

         The simulator would allow engineers and technicians to develop and test the design
parameters of a two-stage compressor station before they were built. Engineers could also
practice decision-making related to the startup and shutdown of a compressor station.
Additionally, the simulated nature of the training environment allows operators to experience
different possible outcomes associated with their decisions, allowing them to learn in a low-risk
setting.

         Overall, a two-stage compressor station startup training simulator will likely help
improve operator training efficiency and increase operator confidence in the event of real-world
emergencies. The two-stage compressor station training simulator should include the ability to
simulate a variety of failure modes, including loss of power to either stage of the compressor,
loss of cooling water flow to either stage of the compressor, and loss of compression head due to
excessive discharge temperature. Additionally, it should be able to demonstrate how each failure
mode affects the overall behavior of the compressor station.

1. Project Overview

1.1 Background and Motivation

         The oil and gas industry continues to lose more than 10 Canadian oil and gas workers
every year due to on-site fatalities (Energy Safety Canada). The effort to improve this number
can never be stopped until injury and fatalities are no longer a risk factor. With that being said,
the majority of accidents occur due to simple mistakes or lack of knowledge regarding the
equipment.

         Operator training needs to constantly evolve and upgrade in an effort to reduce casualties
and losses as its value transcends economic benefit (Assessing the Impact of Simulation-Based
Training on Clinical Skills Acquisition and Retention: A Systematic Review, 2024; Elendu et al.,
2024). Some of the biggest challenges limiting operator training are: high costs associated with
on-site training, production disruptions during training sessions, safety risk during emergency
procedures, limited opportunity for hands-on-experience, limited knowledge retention in
traditional training methods.

         Virtual reality technology revolutionizes operator training by creating an immersive,
rise-free learning environment for skill development and procedure practice (Muda et al., 2024;
Puranik, 2024).

1.2 Problem Statement

         Current operator training methods in the oil and gas industry are inadequate for preparing
personnel for complex operational scenarios and emergency situations. Traditional
classroom-based training lacks practical engagement, while on-site training poses safety risks
and operational disruptions. There is a critical need for an immersive, scalable, and intelligent
training solution that can simulate real-world conditions while ensuring operator safety and
competency.

2. Conceptual Design

2.1 Background Research and Literature Review

2.1.1 Compressor Background Research
         Natural gas produced at the wellhead is typically at insufficient pressure for pipeline

transport, requiring centrifugal compressors at gathering and booster stations to raise gas
pressure before it enters transmission infrastructure (Rasmussen & Kurz, 2009; NETL, 2017).
Centrifugal machines are the standard selection for these applications due to their suitability for
continuous high-flow service. This directly informed the VR OPS scenario selection: the
simulated station replicates a Montney booster application in which a two-stage centrifugal
compression train raises gas pressure from 1.5 MPa at the wellhead gathering system to 8.0 MPa
for export, a configuration that is representative of the upstream and midstream operations for
which operators require training.

         Temperature management is about more than improving performance, but rather, it is
about maintaining reliability as well. Reliability is important for both the process scenario and
the training objectives. The literature showcases a real compressor-process example where a
problem relating to abnormal bearing-temperature behavior resulted in a serious reliability risk
(Lee et al., 2017). The study focused on determining the cause of abnormally high temperatures
associated with a tilting-pad journal bearing. Suh et al. discuss the mechanisms behind that
concern: thermal boundary conditions surrounding bearings influence lubricant properties (oil
viscosity, oil-film pressure, minimum film thickness), pad temperature, and ultimately, bearing
operation (Suh et al., 2020). Therefore, higher-than-normal compressor system temperatures can
degrade the operating conditions of rotating equipment (i.e., bearings and lubricating systems)
potentially resulting in operational instability or increased vibration and decreased machinery
reliability.

         Reliability and availability are prominent topics within the general area of compression
literature. Due to the necessity for compressor stations to continuously provide services,
numerous authors have discussed the utilization of multiple compressor units to improve
redundancy, to continue to provide throughput during outages, and to continue providing service
through maintenance or partial equipment failure. According to NETL (2017), stations
frequently contain multiple compressors so that even when one unit is not available, additional
capacity may be online. Literature on compressor-station availability defines equipment
redundancy as an operational and planning variable in addition to being viewed as a luxury. The

                                                                                                                    9
literature defines reliability by defining machinery protection at the unit level, and continuity of
service at the station level (NETL, 2017; Rasmussen and Kurz, 2009).

         Taken together, the literature treats gas compression as a system-level engineering
problem rather than a collection of independent components. The interdependence between
compression stages, intercoolers, scrubbers, and recycle loops means that a change at any one
point propagates through the entire station. This finding directly shaped the modeling approach
for VR OPS: rather than representing the compressor in isolation, the HYSYS model captures
the full compression train including interstage cooling, liquid separation, and recycle flow paths.
The two-compressor configuration also reflects the redundancy design philosophy described in
the reliability literature, where multiple units allow continued operation during maintenance or
partial failure, a scenario that operators must understand before working on a live station (NETL,
2017; Rasmussen & Kurz, 2009).

2.1.2 Control Panel and VR Research
         For VR OPS, the control interface was a central research consideration because
compressor operations require more than visual recognition of system state. Operators must
execute start and stop actions, adjust valve settings, and respond to abnormal conditions through
deliberate physical inputs. As a result, the literature review focused on whether standard VR
controllers were sufficient for this type of training, or whether a tangible interface would provide
a more appropriate basis for operator interaction (Muda et al., 2024).

                                                                                                                   10
         A key study informing this decision was Baek et al. (2021), who investigated VR-based
job training using tangible interactions. Their results showed that trainees using physical tools
improved their proficiency over time, while those using standard VR controllers did not
demonstrate the same sustained improvement. The authors attributed this difference to the fact
that controller-based interaction did not require the same force control or posture correction as
physical tool use. For VR OPS, this finding was directly relevant because compressor startup
depends on repeated physical actions involving switches, rotary adjustments, and emergency
controls rather than passive observation alone.

         That same study also highlighted an important limitation. Although tangible interaction
improved training quality, the system relied on multiple Kinect sensors for tracking, which
increased setup complexity and reduced portability. This was significant for VR OPS because it
suggested that the value of physical interaction should be preserved, but without depending on an
external tracking arrangement that would complicate deployment. The literature therefore
supported a self-contained physical interface rather than a sensor-heavy tool-tracking system
(Baek et al., 2021).

         The research also showed that interface quality affects training performance
independently of the simulation environment itself. As summarized in the project materials,
Bezanson et al. (2023) found that virtual controls, especially knobs, created usability issues for
many users, with 52% reporting difficulty due to poor sensitivity. In a procedure that depends on
controlled adjustment, this matters because awkward input can interfere with the trainee's ability
to perform the task correctly. Their results therefore strengthened the case for a dedicated
physical panel by showing that controller-based interaction may introduce its own training
barrier even when the virtual environment is otherwise effective.

         In addition to informing the need for the use of physical interaction, the research results
indicated that this needed to be accompanied by meaningful feedback. The findings reinforced
the importance of the control panel being utilized in conjunction with a larger training
framework, where both physical actions taken at the control panel would be related to the
simulator's response to those actions; and the procedures associated with taking those physical
actions would be clearly provided. While the literature supported the utilization of a control
panel as a hardware component of an operator-training program; it did so primarily as an element
of a larger operator-training system. (Baek et al., 2021; Bezanson et al., 2023).

         The performance metrics for VR OPS were established to assess both technical system
performance and training effectiveness. Because the platform integrates a physical control panel,
a virtual reality simulation environment, a process-response model, an AI instructor, and a
supervisory dashboard, its evaluation must address both engineering functionality and trainee
learning outcomes. The selected metrics therefore measure whether the system operates reliably,
produces realistic process behavior, delivers accurate instructional support, and contributes to
measurable improvement in procedural execution over repeated sessions.

2.3 Design Specifications and Constraints

         The VR OPS system was designed to address a specific operator training gap: the need
for a safe, repeatable, and realistic training platform that develops physical motor control,
procedural adherence, and situational awareness for industrial compressor operations. To achieve
this, the design had to satisfy a set of functional and technical specifications while remaining
feasible within the academic project scope. These specifications define what the system must do,
while the constraints define the practical limits that shaped the final concept.

2.4 Development and Descriptions of Design Alternatives

         Two primary design pathways were considered for VR OPS. Both retained the same core

and hardware-software interface mapping, but directly replicates the physical interaction pattern
of a real compressor control station.

2.6 Discussion and Justification of Selected Design Concept

         The hybrid physical panel and VR environment was selected over the full VR alternative
on the basis of interaction fidelity and alignment with the training objective. The primary goal of
VR OPS is procedural competency and abnormal-state recognition, outcomes that benefit
directly from physical muscle memory developed through repeated interaction with real controls.
A software-only interface cannot replicate the psychomotor experience of locating and actuating
an emergency stop under stress. The hybrid concept was therefore selected despite its higher
implementation effort, because the added complexity was justified by the training outcome it
enables.

booster station practice (Rasmussen & Kurz, 2009). The overall pressure ratio of 5.33 is too high

         The panel was designed as a three-module chassis of 3D-printed PLA shells and stencils,

a generic gaming interface.

2.6.3 VR Environment and Feedback Logic
         The compressor station was reconstructed in Unity with dynamic pipe pressure coloring

as the primary process feedback mechanism: grey for no flow, yellow for below range, red for
above range, and green for within the acceptable range at the active step. Compressor audio
scales with dial position and intercooler activation are indicated by flashing status lights,
providing simultaneous multi-modal feedback without requiring trainees to consult numerical
displays. Teleportation points allow navigation between plant areas without motion sickness.

2.6.4 Failure and Reset Logic
         Each startup step has an associated acceptable pressure band at monitored streams. Any

out-of-range condition immediately disables all control inputs, the trainee cannot self-correct by
adjusting valves. Recovery requires pressing the physical ESD, which resets only the active step
while preserving all previously completed steps. This design forces two competencies
simultaneously: recognition that a procedural deviation constitutes an abnormal condition, and
the psychomotor habit of actuating the emergency stop under pressure.

2.6.5 AI Instructor and Supervisory Dashboard
         The AI Instructor answers trainee voice queries in real time using a RAG pipeline

constrained to approved SOP documents, and declines to respond when no relevant context
exists. This keeps procedural guidance traceable to vetted content rather than generated from
general training data. The admin dashboard extends the platform from a demonstration tool to a
full training system by recording per-step timing and correctness for each session, allowing
supervisors to identify recurring weaknesses across multiple attempts rather than reviewing only
final completion status.

         Together, these subsystems form a coherent training architecture in which each layer,
process data, physical input, virtual environment, instructional support, and performance
tracking, has a distinct and non-overlapping role.

3.2 Compressor Simulation (Aspen HYSYS)

3.2.1 Simulation Objectives
         The Aspen HYSYS model has been used to develop a process-level representation of a

staged natural gas compression station. The primary goal for using the simulation was to be able
to determine how the station's pressure, temperature, phase behavior, and recycle conditions
would change when an operator changed one or more of the operator-controlled variables, i.e.
suction control, recycle valve opening, compressor load, and the route that the compressed gas
was exported out of the plant. Therefore, the model was designed to reflect the thermodynamics
and hydraulics of the entire compression train, including all the interactions between each stage
of compression, the coolers, separators, and recycle loops that exist throughout the station, which
is similar to how most references treat compression stations as fully integrated process systems
vs. separate units (Amin et al., 2021; Rasmussen & Kurz, 2009).

         A second objective of the simulation was to show how pressure builds up and gas flow is
redistributed through staged compression. As such, modeling must resolve pressure increase

                                                                                                                   26
across each centrifugal compressor; temperature rises due to compression; coolant-to-gas
temperature differentials across each cooler; separation of phases within each scrubber; and
effect of return gas through anti-surge & full system recycle paths. These goals are consistent
with multistage theory for compression, which treats the suction state of the first stage of
compression, discharge state of the last stage of compression, intercooler losses, and efficiency
of individual stages as the variables that will determine the total performance of the station and
work required to compress the gas (Lugo-Mķndez et al., 2021).

         A third objective is to provide valid physical operating points as reference points in order
to identify safe, warning, and unsafe ranges for the compressor station. Instead of assigning an
arbitrary value for high/low system pressure based upon either a manual or empirical assignment,
the use of simulation enabled the creation of a physically grounded relationship between
movement of valves/compressor control systems and resulting pressures within the overall
system. In doing so this aligns with the stated VR OPS modelling approach in which the HYSYS
model has been utilised to develop a large database of pressures in order that the downstream
behaviours of the system remain directly related to understandable responses to changes in
pressure rather than simply being defined by manual assignments.

3.2.2 Process Flow and Input Parameters
         The processing sequence begins in the suction side of the system at the entry point for the

gas, which goes into a pressure control and mixing region. Following this area the gas is sent to
Scrubber-1; from here it will be subjected to removal of free liquid and then go on to receive its
first stage of compression. Upon completing the first stage of compression (Compressor-1) the
discharged gas will either move onward through Cooler-1 and proceed into an interstage section
or will have been diverted through ASV-1 and returned to the suction side as a means to maintain
a constant flow rate of the compressors. At that time, the forward flowing gas stream will pass
through Cooler-1 and will enter Scrubber-2. In Scrubber-2, free liquid formed by interstage
cooling will be separated from the gas prior to it being compressed once again. For the second
stage of compression, the logic is virtually identical to the first stage. Gas that has exited
Compressor-2 will either proceed onward through Cooler-2 to ultimately leave the station or if
necessary it will be recirculated through ASV-2 back to the suction side for protection of the
equipment when operating at low flow rates. Once gas exits Cooler-2, it may exit via the export
valve or it may exit via the full system recycle valve back toward the inlet side of the station.

         Both the initial conditions of feed streams and the operating conditions of unit operations
were used to define the HYSYS model. The inlet gas stream had an inlet temperature of 25░C ,
an inlet pressure of 1.5 MPa and a flow rate of 1000 kmol/hr. Following suction side control, the
gas passed through the first scrubber and the first-stage compressor at approximately 25░C and
1.45 MPa. The first-stage compressor was modeled using a pressure ratio of 2.4 and a
compression efficiency of 75%. This produced a discharge stream at approximately 114░C and
3.48 MPa. The gas was then cooled in the interstage cooler to approximately 31░C, while the
corresponding pressure was 3.44 MPa at the inlet to the second-stage compressor. The

                                                                                                                   27
second-stage compressor was similarly modeled using a pressure ratio of 2.4 and a compression
efficiency of 75%, resulting in a final discharge stream at approximately 127░C and 7.9 MPa.
Following final cooling, the gas temperature decreased to approximately 35░C while maintaining
a final delivery pressure of approximately 7.9 MPa.

         The initial gas composition is: N = 0.0066; CO = 0.1298; CH = 0.4477; CH = 0.1281;
CH = 0.0640; i-CH = 0.0576; n-CH = 0.0510; i-CH = 0.0455; n-CH = 0.0381; and
n-CH = 0.0315 on a mole fraction basis. This composition represents a relatively rich natural
gas compared to a lean methane-dominant stream, allowing for the generation of meaningful
condensate formation in the scrubbers post-cooling.

         The simulation utilizes steady-state assumptions as described below: steady-state
operation implies no mass accumulation nor energy accumulation in equipment and constant
flow rates, pressures, temperatures, and compositions at each inlet are maintained for all
operating cases. Compressors were modeled using the Schultz polytropic method with a fixed
efficiency rather than utilizing full-vendor compressor performance curves. HYSYS will
calculate the heat duties for the compressors and coolers based upon user-defined pressure-ratios
and cooling objectives. Piping is assumed to be adiabatic and therefore no ambient heat loss or
gain can occur between major pieces of equipment. Scrubbers were modeled as ideal
vapor-liquid separators. Although these assumptions reduce complexity associated with
modeling, they preserve the most significant thermodynamic and hydraulic aspects required for
compressor analysis.

3.2.3 Compressor Staging and Operating Logic
         The simulated station was arranged as two compression stages separated by intercooling

and liquid separation. This configuration was selected because it reflects typical field practice
and distributes the required pressure rise across two machines instead of a single high-ratio stage.
In the model, gas first passes through the suction-side control elements and scrubber, is
compressed in Stage 1, cooled and separated before entering Stage 2, and is then routed either to
export or back through recycle depending on the operating condition. Interstage cooling is also
beneficial because lower compressor inlet temperature reduces compression work relative to a
hotter second-stage inlet (S. Jarung Thammachote; 2022).

         Within the training model, the primary manipulated variables are valve opening
percentages and compressor operating setpoints. These inputs change the flow split between
forward flow and recycle flow, which in turn changes the pressures at the main process streams.
The operator's responsibility in the simulator is therefore to establish a stable suction path, start
compression in the correct sequence, maintain sufficient recycle during low-flow conditions, and
transition the station from recycle-dominant startup to export service without driving either
compressor toward surge. This operating philosophy is consistent with standard
centrifugal-compressor practice, where recycle flow is used during startup, shutdown, and surge
prevention to maintain adequate through-flow across the machine.

3.2.4 Failure-State / Abnormal Scenario Modeling
         The simulation incorporates a structured failure-state framework tied directly to the

compressor startup procedure. Each step in the startup sequence is assigned an acceptable
pressure range at one or more key streams. This range defines the band of steady-state pressures
produced when the correct valves have been set to the correct opening percentages for that step.
Provided all monitored stream pressures fall within their defined bounds upon completion of a
step, the system advances to the next stage of the procedure.

         A failure state is triggered when the pressure at any monitored stream falls outside the
acceptable range for the current step. This condition indicates that one or more valves have been
incorrectly positioned, producing an operating point that deviates from the intended startup
procedure. Upon failure detection, the VR platform immediately disables all control panel inputs.
The trainee cannot correct the error by adjusting valves, the system is intentionally unresponsive
to further input. Recovery requires the trainee to press the emergency shutdown (ESD) button on
the physical control panel, which resets the current step and restores the system to its state at the
start of that step.

         This design is deliberately non-forgiving at the moment of error. A pressurized system in
an abnormal state demands a formal, controlled response, not incremental correction. Research
on abnormal situation management in process industries has consistently shown that operators
who are only trained in normal procedures perform poorly when deviations occur, because they
lack practiced decision pathways for recovery (I. Nimmo; 1995). By forcing the ESD response
rather than allowing valve correction, the training scenario reinforces two things simultaneously:
recognition that a procedural deviation constitutes an abnormal condition, and the physical habit
of locating and actuating the emergency stop, a critical psychomotor skill in field operations.

3.2.5 Integration with VR OPS Platform
         Integration between HYSYS and the VR OPS platform was implemented using an offline

case-study workflow rather than continuous live co-simulation. A large HYSYS case study was
used to sweep the independent variables over the intended operating envelope, including valve
opening percentages and compressor operating setpoints from 0 to 100%. This generated a large
csv. file with many operating points. For each operating point, the model returned the dependent

                                                          31
variables required by the VR environment, primarily the pressures at the key streams and
equipment boundaries.

         The resulting dataset was exported and loaded into Unity as the process-response layer
for the training simulator. During runtime, user inputs from the physical control panel are
mapped to the corresponding operating point, or nearest valid operating region, and the VR scene
updates the process indicators, alarms, and equipment state accordingly. This architecture
preserves a physics-grounded relationship between operator action and process response without
requiring HYSYS to run continuously in the background. That trade-off was intentional. For this
project, the main training objective is procedural correctness and abnormal-state recognition, not
high-frequency transient prediction. An offline dataset therefore provides repeatable scenarios,
low runtime latency, and stable VR performance while still allowing trainees to rehearse
hazardous operating situations in a safe and repeatable environment
3.2.6 Design Rationale
         The HYSYS model was configured to prioritize clear operator-response trends and stable
simulator integration rather than full transient fidelity. Valve behavior was represented using
quick-opening characteristics so that small initial valve movements produced a strong early
change in flow. This was selected because it created a more responsive training model for startup
and recycle actions, and it is consistent with quick-opening valve behavior, where a relatively
small lift near the closed position can produce a large fraction of the maximum flow (Spirax
Sarco; 2026).

                                                                                                                   32
                   Figure 7: Quick-opening flow characteristic curve for the recycle valve.

         For the recycle branches, directly varying recycle-valve opening in steady-state did not
always produce the expected redistribution of flow. To obtain the intended operating response,
the recycle tee split ratios were therefore adjusted to reflect valve opening and closing. This
ensured that opening a recycle path sent more flow through the recycle loop and reduced the
amount of pressure propagated downstream, while closing the recycle path directed more flow
forward through the compression train.

         A Set operation was also introduced in the recycle loops to maintain pressure consistency
before the recycle stream re-entered the mixer. In Aspen HYSYS, Set is a steady-state logical
operation used to enforce a specified relationship between two variables, and it can be used to
maintain one stream pressure relative to another. Here, it was used so the recycle-stream pressure
matched the upstream pressure condition closely enough for stable remixing and steady-state
convergence.

         Overall, these modeling choices were made so that operator inputs produced repeatable,
physically interpretable pressure responses in the simulator. That was more valuable for this
training application than pursuing a more complex dynamic implementation with higher
computational cost and less stable VR integration.

3.3.2 Electrical Design and Communication

3.3.2.1 Electrical Components and Wiring
         The embedded electronics of the control panel are built around the ESP32-WROOM-32E

module that is mounted on a Freenove Breakout Board. This microcontroller runs a dual core
240 MHz processor, It provides sufficient GPIO pins for direct input handling and supports I2C
natively. I2C is a two-wire serial communication protocol that uses a shared data line (SDA) and
a clock line (SCL) to allow the ESP32 to interface with external peripheral devices. By
connecting the MCP23017 I/O expander over the I2C bus, the system gains 16 additional digital
input channels, extending the ESP32's effective GPIO capacity to accommodate all panel inputs.
The breakout board is a printed circuit board (PCB) with labeled header pins that are mapped to
the pins of the microcontroller that is mounted on it, in this case, the ESP-32. To connect wires to
the ESP32, the breakout board has screw terminal blocks, this allows stripped wires to be
inserted and secured using a clamping mechanism from the screw. This mechanism creates a
reliable and stable electrical connection to the ESP32 without having to solder anything. The
ESP32 module, and wires can easily be replaced or changed, making this system modular.

         To power the ESP32, the module can be connected through a USB Type-C connector that
can either be connected directly to a PC or an external power supply. When powered, an onboard
regulator on the ESP32 steps the 5V input down to 3.3V, which runs all the electronics including
the MCP23017, encoders, buttons, and the E-STOP. If the 5V input is required, the module has a
5V rail that is accessible to use for different inputs that require that voltage. All components
share a common ground that is tied to the ESP-32 GND pin. To design the full circuit, the
schematic tool, KiCAD was used.

3.3.2.2 Sensor / Switch / Actuator Selection
         The goal of the control panel is to allow for tactile interaction, this enables users to gain

hands-on experience to get familiar with the look and feel of an actual control panel. The input
devices were chosen to match that physical feel of real industrial compressor control stations.

                                                                                                                   38
The full control panel is split into three sections, labeled S1, S2, and S3. The electronic
components on the first and second section are similar, as it includes two push buttons, and 5
rotary encoders. The last section, S3, includes one E-STOP button and 4 rotary encoders. So all
in all, there are 14 encoders, 4 push buttons and one E-STOP.

         A rotary encoder is an input device that provides rotational control, it generates digital
signals that corresponds to angular movement or simply, it sends signals whether or not the knob
is rotated clockwise or counter clockwise, thanks to the two digital signals CLK and DT. These
two signals have a 90 degree offset between the two channels alowing firmware to determine
both the direction and movement of each turn. The rotary encoder handles all the variable
adjustments, on the simulation it adjusts valve opening percentage, and compressor speeds.
When the rotary encoder is rotated, it sends a message, either 0 or 1, this corresponds to decrease
and increase respectively. Hence, rotary encoders were chosen over potentiometer because it
supports infinite rotation that produces clean digital quadrature outputs with incremental control
rather than absolute position mapping. Rotating the encoders simulates different controls to the
simulation, with little to no latency, allowing for a realistic experience for the operator/trainee.

         The push buttons were also chosen for the same tactile feel, it handles discrete binary
inputs. They are color coded as green and red, for start and stop. These buttons are momentary
normally-open switches that close only when they are pressed. This matches the logic of motor
starter circuits on compressor stations.

         Finally the E-STOP is a mushroom-head latching button. When it is pressed, the button
stays down and locked, it releases only when it is twisted. Thee E-STOP is one of the inputs that
physical connection users will get, they have to locate and engage it under stress, helping the
users psychomotor skills. Traditionally, E-STOPs are wired using normally-closed switches, so it
only sends binary inputs to the simulation in Unity, where the unity program disables all the
panel inputs virtually.

3.3.2.3 Microcontroller and Interface Logic

  Designing the full electronic circuit required a lot of planning and design. The ESP32 has

in total 34 GPIO pins, but only 14 of those pins are useable and safe to use and 6 additional pins

that function as boot or strapping pins and must be handled carefully during startup. The

remaining pins are reserved internally for flash memory, and cannot be used. Due to these

constraints, the circuit had to be planned carefully. Each encoder has 3 pins, two are digital

output pins (A and B) that indicate the rotation (either right or left), and one GND pin. So in

total, 14 ū 2 = 28 pins for input. Four push buttons that only have one input pin and one GND

pin, so 4 pins, and finally the E-STOP that uses one pin for input as well. In total, the system

requires 33 input pins. 

  Since the ESP32 only has around 20 useable pins for I/O, the MCP23017 expander was

chosen to increase the GPIO selection, allowing for 16 more inputs. The MCP23017 has A0, A1,

A2, which are address selection pins that is used to configure the device's I2C address, it has the

SDA and SCL connected to pins 21, and 23 on the ESP32. When an input on the MCP changes,

                                             39
it sends an 8 bit address to the ESP32 through the I2C protocol. Eight of the 14 encoders are
connected to the MCP23017, so only 6 encoders will be connected to the ESP32, decreasing the
load to only 17 pins, which is below the maximum amount.

         The I2C bus uses GPIO 21 and 23 on the ESP32 to connect to SDA and SCL on the MCP
respectively. Both of these lines have pull-up resistors to maintain signal integrity. The
MCP23017 also has a RESET pin that connects to the ESP32 so the firmware can reset the
expander without a new power cycle. The expander has a 0.1 ĄF decoupling capacitor across
VDD and GND to handle voltage transients. To maintain low noise on the I2C bus, the wiring of
the loop area between power and ground return paths were kept small.

3.4 VR Simulation

3.4.1 Virtual Environment Design
         The VR simulation was developed in Unity using the Meta XR SDK, targeting the Meta

Quest headset. The environment replicates a two-stage compressor station, including scrubbers,
compressors, coolers, interconnecting pipework, and valve assemblies, providing trainees with a
spatially accurate representation of the equipment they will operate in the field.

         Locomotion is implemented via a teleportation system rather than continuous joystick
movement. This decision was made to eliminate motion sickness, which is a well-documented
usability barrier for users new to head-mounted displays and would interfere with training focus.
Fixed teleport points are placed at locations corresponding to each major equipment area, suction
side, interstage section, and export side, allowing trainees to reposition themselves to view
equipment relevant to the current procedure step.

                                                                                                                   42
         UI panels displaying the task board, pressure gauges, and AI Instructor interface are
spatially anchored in the environment but can be repositioned and resized by the trainee. This
accommodates different working styles and ensures that panels do not obstruct the equipment
view during critical procedure steps. All panels remain visible and functional regardless of the
trainee's teleport position within the scene.

3.4.2 SOP State Machine and Procedure Logic
         The simulation is structured around a 12-step standard operating procedure (SOP) for

compressor startup. Each step is managed by a central SOPManager state machine, which tracks
the current procedure state, enforces step-by-step sequencing, and validates completion
conditions before allowing progression. Steps require both correct physical inputs and a manual
confirmation action which reduces the chance of accidental advancement.

3.4.3 Physical Control Panel Integration
         Physical controls are connected to an ESP32 microcontroller on the control panel

connected via WiFi. Rotary encoders map to valve controls (SPV, ASV1, ASV2, NRV1, NRV2,
BD1, BD2, and compressor percentages), and buttons handle compressor toggle events. All
hardware inputs are registered through a central InputState registry, which decouples the raw
ESP32 messages from the procedure logic. This design allows the SOP conditions to be
evaluated independently of how or when inputs arrive.

3.4.4 Debug Window and Development Tooling
         To support development and testing when the physical control panel was not available, a

debug window was built into the simulation. This overlay panel exposed all of the same inputs
that the ESP32 hardware would normally provide, including sliders for each encoder-mapped
valve and toggle buttons for the compressors. Any input made through the debug window was
fed directly into the same InputState registry as the hardware signals, meaning the full procedure
logic and pipe color system behaved identically regardless of the input source.

3.4.5 Pipe Pressure Visualization and Feedback

         Pipe segments in the scene change color dynamically based on the current pressure state.
Grey indicates zero pressure, yellow indicates below the required range, red indicates above it,
and light green indicates the pipe is within the acceptable range for the current step. This

3.4.6 Timing Constraints and Step Validation

         Timing constraints are enforced at the step level to prevent trainees from passing through
steps with momentary correct inputs that do not reflect deliberate procedure execution. Valve
transitions require a minimum hold time of 4 seconds at the target position before the step

                                                                                                                   44
condition is considered satisfied. This mirrors the physical behavior of real compressor station
valves, which require controlled, gradual adjustment rather than instantaneous repositioning. The
Step 6 caution note in the SOP (VR-OPS-001) explicitly states that ASV-1 shall not be adjusted
abruptly, and the 4-second hold requirement enforces this constraint directly in the simulator.
Steps also require a manual confirmation action after all input and pressure conditions are met,
further reducing the chance of accidental advancement through the procedure.

3.5 AI Instructor

3.5.1 System Overview
         The AI Instructor is a conversational assistant integrated into the training system that

provides guidance to trainees during the simulation. Rather than relying on a static scripted hint
system, the assistant uses a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) pipeline to answer
questions and deliver relevant procedural information based on the trainee's current state.

3.5.2 RAG Architecture
         RAG works by combining a vector-based document retrieval system with a large

language model. When a trainee submits a question or the system detects that guidance is
needed, the pipeline first retrieves the most relevant passages from a curated knowledge base.
This knowledge base includes the SOP documentation, equipment manuals, safety guidelines,
and operational notes specific to the compressor startup process. The retrieved context is then
passed to the language model alongside the query, allowing the model to generate a grounded,
accurate response rather than relying solely on general training data.

         The pipeline uses nomic-embed-text running locally via Ollama for document
embedding, with ChromaDB as the vector store. Generation is handled by GPT-4o-mini via the
OpenAI API. Document chunks are sized to capture sufficient procedural context for multi-step
questions, with the top-k most relevant chunks retrieved per query and passed to the model
alongside the trainee's question. The model is instructed to answer only from provided context
and to decline when no relevant passage exists.

3.5.3 Trainee Interaction and Voice Input
         The trainee interacts with the assistant through voice input. Speech is captured and

converted to text in real time after the user presses the "Record" button using their finger, then
submitted as a query to the RAG pipeline. The system retrieves the most relevant document
chunks from the knowledge base, ranks them by relevance, and passes them along with the query
to the language model. The generated response is returned and displayed to the trainee as text
within the VR interface. Alongside the response, the source chunks used to produce it are also
surfaced, showing the trainee exactly which part of the SOP documentation or equipment manual
the answer was drawn from. This transparency is important in a safety-critical training context,

                                                                                                                   45
as it allows trainees to verify the information and follow up by consulting the original source if
needed, rather than treating the assistant as a black box.
3.5.4 Knowledge Base Maintenance and Safety

         This approach has several practical advantages for an industrial training context. The
assistant is constrained to information that has been vetted by subject matter experts at the
organization, reducing the risk of the model hallucinating and providing inaccurate information.
It also means the knowledge base can be updated independently of the model itself, for example
when procedures are revised or new equipment is introduced, without requiring retraining or
fine-tuning of the LLM.

3.6 Admin Monitoring Dashboard

         The Admin Monitoring Dashboard is a Streamlit web application served from the
UCalgary CloudStack server. It reads session data from PostgreSQL via PostgREST HTTP
endpoints and provides training coordinators with two core functions: trainee performance
monitoring and AI Instructor knowledge base management. Both functions are accessible
through a single tabbed interface, eliminating the need for separate tools for training oversight
and content administration.

3.6.1 Dashboard Overview and Supervisory Functions
         The dashboard is organized into two levels that correspond to the two main supervisory

tasks: cohort-level screening and individual-level diagnosis. The upper tab presents an
aggregated performance view across all trainees. Supervisors can filter by trainee name and time
horizon, and toggle the main trend chart between total errors per session and completion time per
session. Two summary indicators automatically surface the current top performer and the trainee
with the highest recent error count, allowing instructors to identify who requires intervention
without manually inspecting individual session records.

         The upper tab also contains the SOP Manager, through which administrators can upload,
review, and delete source documents from the RAG knowledge base. Supported formats are
DOCX and PDF. Uploaded documents are chunked and embedded on the server without
requiring any changes to the AI pipeline or application code, keeping knowledge base
maintenance under administrative control rather than developer involvement.

3.6.3 Design Rationale

         The two-level layout was chosen to separate broad monitoring, where speed of

         The backend infrastructure for VR OPS is hosted on a virtual machine provided through

the University of Calgary's CloudStack platform. CloudStack is a cloud computing platform that
the university operates internally for student and research projects.

commercial provider such as AWS or Azure for this project. First, the platform was available at
no cost through the university, removing any ongoing hosting expenses during development and
deployment. Second, and more importantly for a training system handling institutional data, the
CloudStack environment is hosted entirely within the university's own infrastructure. This means
the system is not dependent on a third-party cloud provider and is not subject to the data
residency concerns that can accompany commercial cloud platforms. Finally, network access to
the server can be restricted to authenticated UCalgary users on the university's network, meaning
only verified students or staff can interact with the system. This mirrors the access control model
of a real industrial company where internal tools are restricted to employees on a corporate
network, which is a realistic constraint for the training scenario being simulated.

3.7.2 Database Design
         The system uses PostgreSQL as its primary database, hosted on the same CloudStack

virtual machine as the rest of the backend. Keeping the database on the same server avoids any
network latency between the application layer and the data store, and simplifies the deployment
since no external managed database service is required.

         The schema is organised into three tables. The trainees table stores a unique record per
trainee. The sessions table records each training run, including the date, total completion time,
and total error count for that attempt. The session_steps table stores the per-step breakdown for

                                                                                                                   50
each session, recording how long each of the 12 steps took and whether it was appraised as
correct or incorrect. A view called performance_wide is also defined, which flattens the step data
into a single row per session to match the column layout used in the admin dashboard and make
reporting queries straightforward.

         Rather than exposing PostgreSQL directly to the application layer, the system uses
PostgREST to serve the database over a REST API. PostgREST is a lightweight server that
automatically generates a fully functional HTTP API from a PostgreSQL schema, eliminating
the need to write any custom database query code. This means the VR simulation and the admin
dashboard can both read and write session data using standard HTTP requests, without any
additional backend logic in between. PostgREST runs as a dedicated systemd service on the
CloudStack server, alongside the FastAPI backend and the Streamlit dashboard, and is
configured to connect through a dedicated read-only role so that the API surface is limited to
only what the application needs.

         Nginx sits in front of all three services and acts as a reverse proxy, routing requests to the
appropriate backend based on the path. External access is restricted to ports 80 and 443, with all
internal service ports kept off the public network.

4.2 Descriptions of Test Scenarios

         The test scenario is grounded in the two governing documents developed for this project:
the Compressor Station Startup Procedure (VR-OPS-001, referred to here as the SOP) and the
VR Environment Indicators and Feedback Guide (VR-OPS-002, referred to here as the VR
Guide). The SOP defines the 12-step startup sequence, the required valve positions, and the
acceptance criteria for each step. The VR Guide defines what the trainee should observe in the
virtual environment, pipe colors, audio cues, intercooler status lights, and emergency reset
behavior, as confirmation that the system is responding correctly. Together, these documents
form the basis against which each test scenario is evaluated.

         Figure 19: Standard Operating Procedure and VR Guide Documents that were fed to the

4.2.1 Normal Operation Scenario
         Objective: Verify that a trainee who executes all 12 startup steps correctly in sequence

receives the expected visual, audio, and procedural confirmations at each step, and that the
system advances without triggering any failure state.

         Procedure: The trainee begins with the simulation loaded and all equipment in standby,
consistent with the preconditions defined in Section 4 of the SOP. The trainee executes each step
in order:

         Steps 1Ł3 establish the initial flow path: scrubbers BD-1 and BD-2 are drained to 100%,
both anti-surge valves ASV-1 and ASV-2 are opened fully, and the feed is opened to 100%. Steps
4Ł5 bring Compressor 1 online by setting the SPV to the 10Ł25% range, starting the compressor,
and then advancing the SPV to 40Ł50%. Steps 6Ł12 complete the staged transition to full
operation: ASV-1 and ASV-2 are reduced incrementally, NRV-1 and NRV-2 are opened in stages,
Compressor 2 is started, and both intercoolers are brought online. The final operating state
requires SPV at 100%, NRV-1 and NRV-2 both fully open, ASV-1 at 5%, ASV-2 at 15%, and
both compressors and intercoolers running.

         Expected system response: At each step, the VR environment should confirm correct
completion through the feedback indicators described in the VR Guide. Process pipe sections
should transition from grey (no flow) to green (pressure within the acceptable range for the
active step) as the startup progresses. Compressor audio should begin immediately on start
command and increase proportionally with dial position. Intercooler status lights should flash red
and blue upon activation. The procedure panel should highlight the active step green and expose
the "Next Step" button only after all required actions for that step are complete. By the final step,
all process pipes in the simulation should display green.

         Pass criteria: The system advances through all 12 steps without triggering a failure state,
all final operating confirmations listed in Section 7 of the SOP are satisfied, and all pipes display
green at step completion.

4.2.2 Failure Detection Scenario
         Objective: Verify that the system correctly identifies a procedural deviation, locks out

further control inputs, and requires the trainee to acknowledge the failure before the step can be
retried.

                                                                                                                   54
         Procedure: The trainee is placed at a defined step in the startup sequence for example,
Step 6 (Reduce ASV-1). Rather than closing ASV-1 from 100% to 50% as specified, the trainee
either moves the valve abruptly rather than over the required 4-second minimum, or adjusts it to
an incorrect position outside the acceptable range. This produces a stream pressure that falls
outside the acceptable bounds defined for that step in the HYSYS dataset.

         Expected system response: As described in Section 8 of the VR Guide, the affected pipe
section changes to red, indicating that the simulated pressure has exceeded the acceptable range
for the active step. The VR platform should disable all further control panel inputs at this point,
the trainee cannot correct the error by re-adjusting the valve. The system remains locked until the
Emergency Stop button on the physical control panel is pressed. The SOP identifies this class of
event explicitly in its General Operating Notes: the ESD is to be used when an abnormal or
unexpected response is observed.

         Pass criteria: The red pipe condition appears within the step in which the deviation
occurs and does not propagate silently to the next step. All control inputs are disabled correctly
on failure detection. The system does not accept any further valve or compressor inputs until the
ESD is activated.

4.2.3 Emergency Stop and Recovery Scenario
         Objective: Verify that pressing the Emergency Stop button correctly resets the active

step, restores all controls, and allows the trainee to retry the step from its initial state.

                                                                    55
         Procedure: This scenario follows directly from the failure state established in Section
4.2.2. Once the red pipe condition is displayed and control inputs are locked, the trainee presses
the ESD button on the physical control panel. The system should reset the current step, not the
entire startup sequence, and restore the equipment state to what it was at the beginning of that
step.

         The trainee then repeats the step: for the Step 6 example, ASV-1 is closed gradually from
100% to 50% over at least 4 seconds as specified in the SOP. The SOP's caution note for this step
explicitly states that this adjustment shall not be performed abruptly, and the test verifies that a
smooth adjustment within the correct range clears the failure condition. The VR Guide confirms
that after reset, the trainee should repeat the step actions and confirm that all expected indicators
return to normal before the system allows progression.

         Expected system response: Pressing ESD resets only the active step. The trainee's
progress through all previously completed steps is preserved. After a correct retry, the pipe color
returns to green, the step highlights as complete, and the Next Step button reappears.

         Pass criteria: The ESD resets the active step and restores control panel inputs. The
system does not roll back to Step 1. A correct retry on the reset step results in successful step
completion without triggering a second failure state. The system advances normally to the next
step.

4.2.4 User Interaction / Training Scenario
         Objective: Verify that the supporting interaction features: the AI Instructor, task board,

pressure gauge window, and teleportation system, function correctly and provide the trainee with
accurate, accessible guidance throughout the startup sequence.

         Procedure: This scenario is conducted in parallel with a normal startup run. At defined
points during the sequence, the trainee exercises each of the four support features described in
Section 3 of the VR Guide.

         For the AI Instructor, the trainee activates the input button, speaks a question relevant to
the current step for example, asking why ASV-1 must be reduced gradually in Step 6, and
reviews the response. The response should be grounded in the SOP and display the source
reference used to generate the answer, consistent with the RAG architecture described earlier in
this report.

         For the task board, the trainee verifies that completed steps are highlighted green and that
the active step number and title match the current position in the SOP. The trainee also confirms
that the board can be repositioned and resized in VR without affecting system state.

         For the pressure gauge window, the trainee confirms that the live readings at key plant
locations update correctly as valve positions are changed, and that the values are consistent with
the pipe color feedback shown in the environment.

         For teleportation, the trainee uses the yellow teleport boxes to move between operating
areas during steps that require interaction with equipment in different sections of the plant.

                                                                                                                   56
         Expected system response: AI Instructor responses are returned within the 5-second
latency target and are traceable to the SOP. The task board accurately reflects step status at all
times. Pressure gauge readings are consistent with the HYSYS dataset output for the current
valve configuration. Teleportation functions without disrupting the simulation state or the
procedure panel display.

         Pass criteria: All four support features operate without error during a complete startup
run. AI responses are grounded and accurate. Task board status matches actual step completion
state at all times. Pressure readings are consistent with pipe color feedback. Teleportation does
not cause any loss of session state.

4.4 Integrated System Results and Discussion

         Integrated system testing confirmed that VR OPS meets its primary design objective: a
consistent, repeatable, and physically grounded environment for training operators on
compressor station startup procedures. With all subsystems connected, operator inputs at the
physical control panel produced correct process state changes, pipe color updates, and procedural
progression across all four test scenarios defined in Section 4.2. The system correctly supported
normal startup operation, abnormal-state detection, emergency stop recovery, and guided
operator interaction through the AI Instructor and task board interfaces. No subsystem
integration failures occurred during the test scenarios.

         The use of a precomputed HYSYS lookup table was validated as an appropriate
architecture for this training application. Pressure state transitions were deterministic and
immediate, with no runtime latency introduced by the process response layer. The step-level
pressure bands derived from the HYSYS dataset produced clear and consistent failure-state

                                                                                    59
triggers -- incorrect valve positions were detected reliably and the input lockout behavior
functioned as specified in all tested cases. The offline dataset approach also provided the
scenario repeatability required for training, ensuring that the same operator inputs always
produce the same system response across sessions.

         Three practical limitations were identified during integrated testing that are consistent
with the constraints defined in Section 2.3.3. First, WiFi communication between the ESP32 and
the Meta Quest was occasionally unstable in environments with high network congestion,
causing transient input delays. The software input simulator served as a functional fallback in
these cases, confirming that the procedure logic and feedback system are not dependent on the
physical hardware connection. Second, the steady-state HYSYS dataset does not capture
transient pressure behavior between operating points, meaning the simulator moves directly
between discrete states rather than animating a continuous process response. This is an accepted
trade-off for this training scope, where procedural correctness and step recognition matter more
than dynamic fidelity. Third, the current implementation is scoped to a single two-stage
compressor startup scenario. The modular architecture with independent SOPManager, CSV
lookup, and InputState registry layers, supports extension to additional procedures without
structural changes, but no additional scenarios were implemented or tested within this project.

         Overall, the integrated system results demonstrate that VR OPS functions as a coherent
training platform rather than a collection of loosely connected subsystems. Each layer, process
data, physical input, virtual environment, instructional support, and performance tracking,
operated correctly in combination and produced the training behaviors specified in the design
requirements.

6.1.2 Safety Impact Analysis

         VR OPS provides a means for trainees to practice and learn the "start-up" procedure of a
compressor without the potential risks involved when practicing the start-up sequence on a "live"
pressurized system. With traditional training methods, new operators either "shadow" an
experienced operator during a live training session on a "real" piece of equipment or they
complete a controlled training exercise on a "real" compressor station under the supervision of an
experienced operator. Both traditional training methods expose the trainee/operator to varying
degrees of risk including, but not limited to, a procedural error during a live training session that
could lead to over-pressurization, damage to the equipment, or even loss of containment. VR
OPS completely eliminates these risks by replacing the "live" training scenario with a simulated
environment where errors will have no physical consequence.

         In addition to removing the risks related to the simulation environment, the system was
designed to provide a specific solution to abnormal situation management (ASM). ASM is an
area that research has shown lacks preparedness in operators. Instead of allowing trainees to
self-correct if a deviation occurs, the system locks all input devices and requires the trainee to
manually locate and activate the Emergency Shutdown (ESD) button. This was done to develop
two competencies simultaneously: recognizing cognitively that a deviation has occurred and
activating the ESD button (a psychomotor skill), which is a critical function in emergency
response and must be practiced in a realistic environment.

         Additionally, enforcing ESD response is an effective method of addressing another
well-known failure mode in operator training. Trainees that are only trained in normal
procedures do not have a defined path of decision-making in recovering from abnormal
conditions. VR OPS integrates failure modes into every training session using the step-level
pressure validation system, ensuring that operators experience and practice emergency response
as part of their normal training regimen instead of just once as an isolated exercise.

                                                                                                                   69
6.1.3 Environmental Considerations

         The environmental considerations were assessed based on both the direct footprint of VR
OPS and the avoided environmental impacts compared to those of conventional training
methods. The direct environmental footprint of VR OPS is minimal. The cloud-based backend of
VR OPS is hosted on a university-provided virtual machine that uses a very small percentage of
the available server resources; the Meta Quest headset draws about 10Ł15 watts during use; and
the ESP32 control panel consumes virtually no power. There are no consumable materials used
during training sessions.

         However, the avoided environmental impacts are much larger. Traditional training
practices at compressor stations may involve powering up compression equipment only for
training purposes, thus utilizing fuel gas, producing emissions, and creating noise pollution. Live
training exercises at each reciprocating or centrifugal compressor station include venting or
flaring during startup and shutdown cycle operations, resulting in additional greenhouse gases
released into the atmosphere. Therefore, by providing a virtual platform for operators to practice
these procedures without having to run physical equipment, VR OPS reduces the number of live
training events needed to obtain operator competency and subsequently reduces fuel usage and
emissions.

6.1.4 Human Factors and Usability Analysis

         Designers of both the physical control panel and the virtual reality (VR) interface
employed human factor principles as they created both interfaces. The design of the control
panel was intended to have all of the physically related controls grouped together in an orderly
fashion; the valve encoder layout was also laid out similarly to the way the piping is laid out at a
compressor station. The emergency stop button was located on the control panel in a prominent
location and at eye level where it could be quickly accessed and activated, which ensured that the
psychomotor reaction that occurred while training would be transferred directly into the actual
operation of the equipment at the compressor stations.

         The designers also utilized many methods to ensure usability within the VR environment.
One method used was the pipe color feedback system. This system provided trainees with
immediate visual feedback regarding whether there was any flow or if the flow rate was outside
of acceptable limits. The color scheme included grey when there was no pressure, yellow when
flow rates were below normal operating ranges, red when pressures were above normal operating
ranges, and green when pressures fell within normal operating ranges. Trainees did not have to
refer to numeric display screens for this information. Two other panels within the VR
environment were also able to be moved by trainees. The panels were the task board and the
pressure gauges. These two panels were made movable in space so that each trainee could
position these panels in his/her field of vision based upon individual preferences for how he/she

                                                                                                                   70
preferred to operate. By providing trainees with flexibility in positioning the panels, designers
reduced the need for head movement to view important information. Movement in the VR
environment was accomplished using a teleportation system instead of continuous movement.
Designers chose to use a teleportation system to reduce the incidence of motion sickness. Motion
sickness has been identified as one of the most common usability concerns associated with VR
systems. Many VR system developers are developing VR applications specifically for
individuals that will be wearing a HMD (Human Mobile Device) for the first time. Teleportation
points were established along the path that would correspond with the typical viewing locations
around a compressor station.

7. Conclusions and Recommendations

7.1 Project Summary

         VR OPS was developed to address a specific gap in oil and gas operator training: the
absence of a safe, repeatable, and physically grounded environment for practicing compressor
station startup procedures. Conventional training methods, live equipment exercises and
experienced-operator shadowing, carry inherent safety risks and operational costs that VR OPS
eliminates by replacing live exposure with an immersive simulation where procedural errors
have no physical consequence.

         All six deliverables defined in Section 1.3 were completed and verified. The two-stage
Montney natural gas compressor simulation was built in Aspen HYSYS and validated against
theoretical staging calculations, with the resulting 100,000-row lookup table driving
process-response behavior in the VR environment. The physical control panel was fabricated as a
three-module PLA chassis housing 14 rotary encoders, 4 push buttons, and a latching ESD,
communicating with Unity over WiFi via TCP. The VR simulation was deployed on a Meta
Quest headset and implements the full 12-step startup SOP, including step-level pressure
validation, failure-state detection, input lockout, and ESD recovery logic. The AI Instructor was
deployed as a RAG pipeline on the UCalgary CloudStack server, providing SOP-grounded
procedural guidance with source citations and declining to respond to out-of-scope queries. The
admin monitoring dashboard records per-step timing and correctness for each session and
exposes this data through a Streamlit interface supporting both cohort-level and individual
trainee analysis. Comprehensive technical documentation was produced in the form of two
governing documents, VR-OPS-001 and VR-OPS-002, which served as both the SOP reference
for the simulation and the primary knowledge base for the AI Instructor.

         The integrated system was verified across four test scenarios covering normal operation,
failure detection, emergency stop recovery, and AI-assisted interaction. All subsystems met their
individual pass criteria and functioned correctly in combination. The prototype demonstrates that
a physically grounded, AI-supported VR training platform can be developed and deployed within
a student-project budget of CAD 450, using open-source infrastructure and university-hosted
compute resources.

7.2 Suggestions for Design Improvements

                                                                                                                   73
         The greatest potential for an enhancement lies in developing the process-response layer
from a static, offline data set into a hybrid or dynamic simulation environment. While the current
static lookup table methodology will consistently produce stable, physically accurate training
responses that can be predicted with high accuracy, if the training were to simulate transient
responses such as startup ramp up/down rates, pressure delay times, and/or time-dependent surge
margins, it would provide much greater realism when simulating abnormal situations and/or
when transitioning between different operating states. A good example of how this could be used
would be to allow operators to identify and respond to early-stage surge indicator signals instead
of simply waiting until they reach an out-of-range condition.

         Adding new procedures to the process manager is the second-highest priority item.
Currently, the process manager supports one single two-stage compressor startup. If you were to
add additional shutdowns, upset scenarios, multi-equipment configurations, and procedures
supporting other facility areas outside of what currently exist within the models boundaries, it
would expand your training capabilities beyond just being a single-scenario trainer to a complete
operator proficiency development tool. In doing so, the independent design structure of each of
the three layers (SOPManager, CSV Lookup Table Manager, and Input State Registry Layers)
was designed to enable this expansion with no structural changes to the existing core system.

         Improving hardware on the physical control panel would improve the overall realism and
durability of the panel. The current PLA case and encoders are fine for a proof of concept but
would greatly benefit from using higher-quality industrial-grade materials and enclosures, as
well as providing more inputs/outputs for additional control elements to support more complex
procedures.

         Lastly, both the AI instructor and administrator dashboards have plenty of opportunities
to enhance their functionality. Enhancing the knowledge base by incorporating multiple
equipment manuals and operational references, enabling feedback based upon past performance
history of trainees, and allowing administrators to compare sessions across trainees on the admin
dashboard would significantly advance VR OPS from its current status as a working prototype
towards a commercially viable and deployable training solution.
